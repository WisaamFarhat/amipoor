Early Retirement Planning

How to retire at 55

Retiring at 55 is the most achievable early retirement target for most people. With a 20-30% savings rate from your late 20s, the numbers work across a wide range of incomes. Many employer pension schemes also allow access from 55 (rising to 57 in the UK), making 55 a natural trigger point.

The numbers behind retiring at 55

How much do you need?

FIRE Number for age 55 retirement = Annual Expenses × 25 At 55, you need a more conservative withdrawal rate than the standard 4% because your retirement could last 35 years or more.

$25,000/year expenses → 25 × $25,000 = $625,000
$50,000/year expenses → 25 × $50,000 = $1,250,000
$75,000/year expenses → 25 × $75,000 = $1,875,000

How long does it take to get there?

If you start at 22 with nothing, invest at 7% returns annually, and maintain a 20-30% savings rate, you can accumulate enough to retire at 55. The exact timeline depends heavily on your income, expenses, and when you start.

The single most powerful lever is starting age. Starting at 22 vs 28 is the difference between retiring at 55 and retiring at 61. Compound interest rewards early starters disproportionately.

What about pension access?

Most state pensions and private pension schemes have a minimum access age (55–67 depending on country and scheme). If you retire at 55, you may have a gap of 10-20 years before pension income kicks in. Your FIRE portfolio needs to bridge this gap. Many early retirees maintain separate "bridge" portfolios in taxable accounts for early retirement years, preserving tax-advantaged accounts for later.

Steps to retire at 55

  • Calculate your FIRE number. Use our FIRE Number Calculator to find your target portfolio based on expected retirement expenses.
  • Know your savings rate. Use our savings rate calculator to see if your current rate gets you there in time.
  • Invest consistently in low-cost index funds. Time in the market beats timing the market. A globally diversified equity index fund at 0.1–0.2% fees compounding over 33 years is the engine of FIRE.
  • Eliminate high-interest debt first. No investment reliably returns more than the interest on credit card debt. Clear it before investing.
  • Maximise tax-advantaged accounts. ISA, TFSA, Roth IRA, NISA — whichever applies to your country. These shelter investment returns from tax, meaningfully accelerating compounding.
  • Keep lifestyle inflation in check. The biggest threat to early retirement is lifestyle creep — spending rising to match income. A raise invested rather than spent is years shaved off your working life.

Frequently asked questions

Is retiring at 55 realistic?
It's more achievable than most people think — but less achievable than FIRE influencers sometimes suggest. It requires above-average income, disciplined saving, and consistent investing for 33+ years. For high earners in low-tax environments (UAE, Singapore), the maths is significantly easier than for average earners in high-tax countries.
What do people who retire at 55 actually do?
Most don't stop being productive — they stop working for money. Common paths include: passion projects, part-time consulting, travel, volunteering, entrepreneurship with no financial pressure, and raising children without the constraint of employment. Financial independence makes work optional rather than mandatory, which often changes the work you choose to do.
What are the risks of retiring at 55?
Key risks include: sequence-of-returns risk (market crash early in retirement), healthcare costs (especially in countries without universal healthcare), unexpected life events (divorce, illness), lifestyle inflation, and the psychological adjustment of leaving a career identity. Planning for these — through conservative withdrawal rates, emergency funds, and flexible spending — significantly reduces their impact.
